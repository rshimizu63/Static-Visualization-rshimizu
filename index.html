Inefficiency in the distribution of medical resources in Japan

In Japan, thanks to the universal health insurance system, all people can access sufficient medical services regardless of their wealth or location. However, since a large portion of medical expenditures is publicly covered, medical resources—such as doctors, hospitals, and hospital beds—are not necessarily distributed efficiently. This can lead to unnecessary expansion of medical expenditures. In this project, using prefecture-level data in Japan, I aim to demonstrate that an excessive concentration of hospital resources in certain prefectures can lead to the provision of unnecessary medical services, thereby increasing medical expenditures, while contributing relatively little to improving people’s health status.

The high number of hospital beds is directly associated with high medical expenditure

Source: Created by the author based on MHLW, Survey of Medical Institutions, National Medical Expenditure

Figure 3 shows the number of hospital beds per capita and medical expenditure in each prefecture. Interestingly, the two variables are clearly highly correlated. This suggests that people in prefectures with many hospital beds bear a heavier burden of medical costs, and it is assumed that an over-allocation of hospital beds may cause some inefficiency.

A similar relationship can be observed between the number of beds and the hospitalization fee per capita(Figure 4). Since the correlation appears to be stronger, it can be assumed that higher medical expenditures are associated with more frequent or longer hospital stays, which may be influenced by the availability of hospital beds.

An abundance hospital beds and high medical expenditure does not necessarily contribute to improve people's health

In figure 7, the bubble size represents healthy life expectancy in deviation score(average = 50). Although the x-axis and y-axis look correlated, the bubble size appears to be completely unrelated to the amount of medical expenditure.

FIgure 8 also describe the relationship among hospital beds, health expenditure and healthy life expectency. Y-axis represents the rank among prefectures about each factors. Since the number of hospital beds and health expenditure are correlated, relatively few lines cross each other. In contrast, healthcare expenditure and healthy life expectency are visibly tangled, indicating little apparent relationship. This suggests that the concentration of healthcare resources does not necessarily lead to improved health outcomes.

Conclusion

As we can observe above, there is a possibility that abundant hospital beds lead to an increase in unnecessary medical service provision, causing an expansion of medical expenditure. Though Japanese people can benefit from the universal health insurance system, they ultimately have to bear the burden of such expenditure. These data provide compelling evidence for the need to optimize the allocation of medical resources.
